Journey Mapping

Developer Experience Atlas

Revealing hidden friction across products during IPO-stage scale

Timeline
2021-2022
Context
Pre-IPO Platform Strategy
Methods
Journey Mapping, Impact Scoring

Initial Situation

As DigitalOcean scaled its product ecosystem in preparation for IPO, a central problem emerged: Developers loved individual products — but weren't adopting more than one.

Despite high satisfaction scores, usage data revealed a gap: multi-product adoption rates were low. Product teams optimized their verticals, but no one owned the horizontal experience.

I was brought in to lead a strategic, cross-functional research initiative to map the end-to-end developer journey, surface transition friction, and build a shared understanding of how platform cohesion was breaking down.

The challenge wasn't improving individual products — it was understanding the spaces between them.

Product Satisfaction

High ratings for individual products

BUT

Cross-Product Adoption

Low usage across multiple products

Research Mission

My research aimed to diagnose where — and why — developers were failing to adopt across product lines.

01

Journey Mapping

Map the real sequence of onboarding, building, scaling, and expanding

02

Friction Identification

Identify the emotional, technical, and support-based friction points invisible to individual teams

03

Cross-functional Alignment

Create an artifact that could align PMs, leadership, and GTM around the real developer journey

04

Opportunity Mapping

Identify high-impact areas for improving cross-product experiences

Methodology

I employed a multi-layered approach to capture the full developer experience across products and time.

1

Longitudinal Journey Mapping

From onboarding to expansion

2

Multi-Layered Experience Modeling

Actions, emotions, support needs

3

Impact Scoring by Friction Type

Severity × frequency × business impact

4

Stakeholder Synthesis Workshops

Cross-team interpretation

Method

Combined product interaction logs with live interviews and diary studies

Participants

25+ developers tracked across 3-month journey periods

Key Finding

The developer lifecycle followed a predictable maturity curve with 5 distinct phases: onboarding, building, scaling, graduation, expansion

Method

Layered feature use, documentation behavior, and support interactions

Participants

30+ developers across product usage patterns

Key Finding

Distinct experience layers (actions, emotions, support) revealed dramatic differences between intended and actual user journeys

Method

Pain point matrix scoring severity, frequency, and business impact

Participants

Quantified 40+ journey friction points

Key Finding

The highest-impact issues lived in transition points between products and "graduation moments" where user needs evolved

Method

Cross-team workshops for interpretation and prioritization

Participants

Product, design, support, and leadership teams

Key Finding

Common understanding of journey issues led to new cross-team collaboration structures

Key Artifact

The Developer Experience Atlas became a foundational alignment tool that revealed critical friction points invisible to siloed teams.

Developer Experience Atlas - Journey Map

Critical Friction Point

Cross-product authentication requiring reauthentication led to 30% abandonment

Team Graduation

30% drop-off at team upgrade point due to forced migration rather than gradual transition

Strategic Opportunity

Cross-product onboarding flow could significantly improve multi-product adoption

Purpose

This map revealed the critical points where developers experienced friction when moving between products. It visualized actions, emotions, support needs, friction points, and opportunities across the journey from onboarding to multi-product expansion.

Impact

This artifact became the foundation for cross-product strategic planning and was utilized in employee onboarding to create a shared understanding of the developer journey. It was cited in IPO materials as evidence of DigitalOcean's developer-centric approach.

Key Insights

The research revealed four fundamental truths about cross-product experiences that were invisible to siloed teams.

Friction Lived Between Products, Not Inside Them

Satisfaction was high within silos — but transition points between products revealed unmet needs, mismatched expectations, and broken workflows.

No One Owned Graduation Moments

As developers matured (e.g., from solo to team), there were critical points where they needed new features — but didn't know they existed, or couldn't adopt them smoothly.

Documentation and Support Were Misaligned with UI Flow

Developers frequently switched between docs, dashboards, and support for guidance — but these systems were out of sync, creating confusion and dropout risk.

Developer Churn Was Preventable — but Invisible

Without a holistic view, teams were missing moments where developers silently disengaged. In one case, a 4-click UI gap led to over 30% drop-off from multi-product usage.

The Developer Experience Atlas fundamentally changed how we think about our platform. Instead of optimizing products in silos, we now optimize for the developer's journey across our entire ecosystem.

— Chief Product Officer

Organizational Adoption

Beyond its immediate impact on product decisions, the Atlas influenced broader organizational changes:

  • Became a standard onboarding artifact for new employees in product, design, and CX
  • Influenced roadmap direction for account management, onboarding, and multi-product UI
  • Led to the creation of a cross-product experience team focused on transition points
  • Highlighted support+docs gaps that led to unified experience planning across previously siloed teams

Reflection

This case exemplified what I believe research is capable of at its best: Surfacing what no one team can see alone — and aligning the entire system around the user's lived reality. By creating a map of the unowned spaces — the in-between — we turned a fragmented set of tools into the foundation for a more cohesive platform.

It wasn't just a research win. It was a cultural shift in how the company thought about experience, responsibility, and growth.
