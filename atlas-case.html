Developer Experience Atlas

Revealing hidden friction across products during IPO-stage scale

Journey Mapping Platform Strategy Cross-Product UX IPO-Stage Research

Initial Situation

As DigitalOcean scaled its product ecosystem in preparation for IPO, a central problem emerged: Developers loved individual products — but weren't adopting more than one. Despite high satisfaction scores, usage data revealed a gap: multi-product adoption rates were low.

Product teams optimized their verticals, but no one owned the horizontal experience. I was brought in to lead a strategic, cross-functional research initiative to map the end-to-end developer journey, surface transition friction, and build a shared understanding of how platform cohesion was breaking down.

Research Mission

Diagnose where — and why — developers were failing to adopt across product lines, by:

  • Mapping the real sequence of onboarding, building, scaling, and expanding
  • Identifying the emotional, technical, and support-based friction that wasn't visible from within any single team
  • Creating an artifact that could align PMs, leadership, and GTM around the real developer journey

Methodology

Phase Objective Method
1. Longitudinal Journey Mapping Track full developer lifecycle from onboarding to expansion Combined product logs with interviews and diary studies
2. Multi-Layered Experience Modeling Map actions, decisions, emotions, and team touchpoints across time Layered feature use, documentation behavior, and support interactions
3. Impact Scoring by Friction Type Create a pain point matrix: severity × frequency × business impact Flagged key breakpoints for churn, support load, and platform expansion
4. Stakeholder Synthesis Workshops Facilitate cross-team interpretation and prioritization Built buy-in and clarity across product, design, support, and leadership

Developer Experience Atlas - Journey Map

Key Artifact

Purpose: This map revealed the critical points where developers experienced friction when moving between products. It visualized actions, emotions, support needs, friction points, and opportunities across the journey from onboarding to multi-product expansion.

Impact: This artifact became the foundation for cross-product strategic planning and was utilized in employee onboarding to create a shared understanding of the developer journey.

Key Insights

Friction Lived Between Products, Not Inside Them

Satisfaction was high within silos — but transition points between products revealed unmet needs, mismatched expectations, and broken workflows.

No One Owned Graduation Moments

As developers matured (e.g., from solo to team), there were critical points where they needed new features — but didn't know they existed, or couldn't adopt them smoothly.

Documentation and Support Were Misaligned with UI Flow

Developers frequently switched between docs, dashboards, and support for guidance — but these systems were out of sync, creating confusion and dropout risk.

Developer Churn Was Preventable — but Invisible

Without a holistic view, teams were missing moments where developers silently disengaged. In one case, a 4-click UI gap led to over 30% drop-off from multi-product usage.

Organizational Influence

Revenue Impact: Increased ARPU by 15% among cohorts exposed to cross-product nudges informed by the Atlas

Organizational Adoption: Integrated into employee onboarding as a strategic artifact for new hires in product, design, and CX

IPO Influence: Cited in IPO S-1 filing as part of the company's understanding of developer lifecycle and engagement

The Developer Experience Atlas influenced roadmap direction for account management, onboarding, and multi-product UI. It highlighted support+docs gaps that led to unified experience planning across previously siloed teams.

Reflection

This case exemplified what I believe research is capable of at its best: Surfacing what no one team can see alone — and aligning the entire system around the user's lived reality. By creating a map of the unowned spaces — the in-between — we turned a fragmented set of tools into the foundation for a more cohesive platform.

It wasn't just a research win. It was a cultural shift in how the company thought about experience, responsibility, and growth.
