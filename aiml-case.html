Platform Strategy

AI/ML Platform Adoption Research

Revealing the real drivers of ML platform adoption beyond infrastructure performance

Timeline
2023-2024
Context
Post-Acquisition Integration
Methods
Mixed-Methods, Contextual Inquiry

Initial Situation

After acquiring Paperspace, DigitalOcean saw an opportunity to expand into the AI/ML space by leveraging its newly acquired GPU infrastructure. The internal assumption was clear: Top-tier hardware would attract ML developers.

As investment planning began, this idea was gaining momentum — backed by a roadmap focused on compute performance, benchmarking, and instance types. But I knew from experience: adoption doesn't begin with specs. It begins with how developers think, learn, and build.

So I reframed the research to ask a more consequential question:

What actually drives ML developers to choose — and stick with — a cloud platform?

Org Assumption

Hardware specs drive platform selection

VS

Research Question

What actually matters to ML developers?

Research Mission

My research was designed to uncover what motivates AI/ML developers at each stage of their workflow.

01

Platform Evaluation

Identify what ML developers look for when evaluating a platform

02

Progress Blockers

Discover what blocks developer progress (both technically and cognitively)

03

Trust Development

Understand what builds trust, fluency, and long-term usage in an ML platform

04

Infrastructure Intersection

Explore how infrastructure capabilities intersect with usability, cost, and support

Surfacing existing assumptions

5

Distilling meaningful adoption patterns

Method

A strong organizational belief that GPU performance would be the primary driver of platform adoption

COM-B behavioral model and thematic clustering

Participants

Success in the first 48 hours was the most reliable predictor of long-term platform adoption

Method

Persona tiering and insight frameworks

Key Finding

Three distinct ML developer personas emerged with different needs: hobbyists, experimenters, and orchestrators

Purpose

This framework juxtaposed organizational assumptions against user research findings to reveal the real drivers of ML platform adoption. It highlighted the gap between expected motivators (hardware specs) and actual motivators (workflow fit).

Impact

This artifact was instrumental in redirecting the product strategy from a hardware-centric approach to one focused on developer momentum and experience. It became a key decision-making tool across the organization.

Key Insights

The research revealed four fundamental truths about ML platform adoption that challenged organizational assumptions.

Workflow Fit > Hardware Specs

Developers didn't just want power — they wanted momentum. If setup, deployment, or scaling felt unpredictable, the power didn't matter.

Documentation Was a Strategic Asset

Even highly skilled ML engineers relied on templates, walkthroughs, and example repos. Developer self-sufficiency beat raw capability.

Support Ecosystem = Trust Layer

Users prioritized access to responsive support, Slack communities, and peers over pricing or brand. Confidence was social and cognitive, not just technical.

First Run Success Was the Real KPI

Platforms weren't judged by marketing claims — they were judged by how fast a user could go from concept to working model without needing to file a support ticket.

Strategic Partnerships

The research findings directly influenced the formation of strategic partnerships with HuggingFace and DeepSeek based on integration and ecosystem benefits rather than hardware capabilities. This dramatically improved the adoption rate of the platform among AI/ML developers who were previously skeptical of a new entrant in the space.

Reflection

This project reinforced a core belief I bring to all infrastructure research: Even the best hardware fails if the developer can't get through day one. My work revealed that DigitalOcean's competitive edge in AI/ML wasn't compute — it was approachability.

And by exposing where adoption was stalling, we empowered the company to invest not in flashier specs, but in the scaffolding developers actually needed to move fast with confidence. This wasn't just a usability win. It was a strategic pivot that made the platform more credible, more usable, and more aligned with how ML is practiced — not just imagined.
